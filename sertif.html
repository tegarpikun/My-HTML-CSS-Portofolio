How we collaborate with engineering and PM teams to create digital solutions

I still remember that day when i still was in the Government. The product manager came with a request: "We need a new wishlist feature, user engagement is declining!" We immediately got excited about designing beautiful components—heart icons, animations, drag-and-drop features. Two weeks of hard work went into it.

The initial design process illustration.

Because of it, as a designer i'm using a Design Thinking Method that from the method we should to know first about what the problem is? So we do the emphatized and for them we held a Meeting. And then during the presentation to the engineering team, a backend developer asked a crucial question: "? From the data I see, 70% of drop-offs occur before users even reach the product page." This was a significant wake-up call. Our collaboration with software engineers and product managers truly began here.

Key Learnings from the Revelation:

  • Always start with Actual Data, not assumptions.
  • Invite All Stakeholders from the very beginning of the project.
  • Practice asking "Why?" at least 5 times to truly get to the root of the problem.

Collaborative Workshop Insights

Following this realization, a pivotal meeting was held again. It included two engineers, one Product Manager, Two Designer include me and three actual users, all connected via Google Meet to not increase the budget. This early user research step was critical. The virtual whiteboard quickly filled with sticky notes bearing valuable insights. This is how UX research methods, such as user interviews and contextual inquiry, were incorporated:

"I want to be able to see my favorite products without having to log in first."

"Why does the wishlist button often get covered by pop-ups?"

"Can I get notifications when my favorite products are on sale?"

workshop session illustration
Image Source: Unsplash.com

After intensive brainstorming among the designers, users, and the product manager, a brilliant idea emerged: "We can use local cache for wishlists, allowing users to save items without logging in. However, notifications can only be sent after a user logs in." This approach balanced user convenience with technical feasibility.

From Ideation to Polished Prototype

The first version of our interactive prototype was, frankly, a complete failure. Five out of seven user testers had significant trouble finding the wishlist button. But thanks to their quick and honest feedback, we were able to iterate and refine the design within just two days.

To ensure we had the best solution, I suggested using **A/B Testing**. We simultaneously tested versions A and B on different user segments, meticulously collecting metrics. The result? The floating wishlist button in the lower right corner won hands down, boasting an impressive 80% engagement rate!

Crucial Takeaways from Prototyping & Testing:

  • Interactive Prototypes are essential for uncovering unexpected UX issues early.
  • A/B Testing provides data-backed solutions, moving beyond assumptions.
  • A fast and cost-effective Iteration process in the Design phase significantly saves budget later.

We initially thought our design task was complete. However, the Engineering Team still needed critical clarifications on various states and behaviors:

"What Loading State will we use for the wishlist?"

"What will happen if wishlist already reach for 100 items?"

"How Behavior for this Design in Ultra Wide Screen?"

Our solution? We developed comprehensive Living Documentation directly within Figma, which eventually fed into our nascent design systems effort. This detailed document covered every edge case, interaction, and responsive behavior, serving as a single source of truth for both design and development. My expertise in HTML and CSS was invaluable in understanding implementation feasibility.
